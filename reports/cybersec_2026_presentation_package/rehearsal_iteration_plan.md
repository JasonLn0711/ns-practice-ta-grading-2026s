# CYBERSEC 2026 Rehearsal And Iteration Plan

Purpose: use the 100-point scoring rubric to improve the talk through structured review cycles, not last-minute slide polishing.

Target readiness: content stable, timing controlled, delivery confident, evidence logic clear, and the closing strong enough to be remembered after the conference.

## 1. Rehearsal Sequence

| Pass | Goal | Output | Gate To Continue |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| First-draft review | Confirm narrative and slide role | Slide-by-slide issue list | Every slide has one message and a reason to exist |
| Second-draft design review | Reduce density and improve diagrams | Visual/action revision list | Main point of each slide visible within three seconds |
| Dry run 1 | Test full timing and story | Scored run with timestamps | Finish content by `30:00` even if rough |
| Dry run 2 | Fix rhythm and transitions | Scored run with cut list | Finish content by `28:30` plus buffer |
| Live rehearsal | Simulate stage pressure | Peer score sheet and final fixes | Score `85+`, no critical issue |
| Final readiness run | Confirm stability | Go/no-go score | Score `90+` or accepted conference-ready risk |

## 3. Stage Rhythm And Delivery Design

### Rhythm Control Plan

| Moment | Slide | Rhythm Move | Purpose |
| --- | ---: | --- | --- |
| Opening line | 1 | Slow, grounded | Establish authority |
| Disclaimer | 2 | Short and neutral | Preserve momentum |
| `你賣的不是模型` | 3 | Pause after line | Let reframing land |
| Trend to incident | 4-5 | Slight acceleration | Build urgency |
| Four-scale map | 6 | Decelerate | Make the map memorable |
| Evidence chain | 7 | Slow and authoritative | Convert regulation into logic |
| FDA/TFDA/NIST | 8-11 | Controlled medium pace | Avoid lecture feel |
| AI stack | 12 | Reset sentence | Prepare audience for product scale |
| Product scales | 13-16 | Repeated pattern, efficient | Make comparison easy |
| Patient safety line | 17 | Silence after line | Create emotional peak |
| White/black-box contrast | 18-19 | Crisp contrast | Prevent tool-list fatigue |
| Patch SLA | 20 | Strong, decisive | Deliver operating climax |
| Small-team playbook | 21 | Conversational | Reduce audience pressure |
| `30/60/90` | 22 | Measured cadence | Make roadmap executable |
| Final line | 23 | Slow, stop cleanly | Preserve final impression |

### Rhetorical Questions

- Slide 6: `如果產品每多一層，風險也多一層，那證據是不是也要跟著多一層？`
- Slide 9: `每個 finding 最後去哪裡了？`
- Slide 20: `如果只能帶走一個營運流程，哪一個最值得？`

### Reset Sentences

- After slide 6: `不要記所有細節，先記四層就好。`
- After slide 8: `Let me translate this back into product work.`
- Before slide 20: `If you remember only one operating process, remember Patch SLA.`
- Before slide 22: `重點不是三個月變完美，而是三個月後公司開始自動留下可信證據。`

### Likely Bad Rhythm Patterns

| Pattern | How To Detect In Rehearsal | Correction |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Reading dense slides | Eyes stay on screen for more than five seconds | Speak the title claim, then one example only |
| Regulatory drag | Slides 7-12 exceed `8:30` | Cut sub-framework explanation; keep evidence chain |
| Scale-section monotony | Slides 13-16 sound identical | Start each slide with what changed at that scale |
| Rushed close | Slide 21 begins after `26:00` | Cut one detail from slides 18-19; preserve slide 23 |
| Overexplaining examples | One example exceeds `45` seconds | Convert example to one sentence plus consequence |
| Weak final stop | Speaker adds extra caveats after final line | Say thanks and stop |

## 4. First-Draft Review Checklist

- [ ] The talk's core thesis appears by slide 3.
- [ ] The four-scale product map appears by slide 6.
- [ ] FDA/TFDA/NIST are translated into evidence workflow, not left as names.
- [ ] Each slide has one controlling message.
- [ ] Every major suggestion names the scoring category it improves.
- [ ] Every dense slide has a one-sentence spoken spine.
- [ ] Slide 17 is a true emotional peak, not another technical list.
- [ ] Slide 20 is the operational climax.
- [ ] Slides 21-23 give action and close without new concepts.
- [ ] No section contains advice that cannot become a slide, timing, delivery, or scoring action.
- [ ] No private client, hospital, student, code, or patent-sensitive content appears.

## 5. Second-Draft Design Review Checklist

- [ ] Main point visible within three seconds on every slide.
- [ ] Architecture and process ideas use diagrams, not paragraph blocks.
- [ ] Color has semantic meaning: risk, layer, decision, or action.
- [ ] No slide requires the audience to read while the speaker is explaining a different point.
- [ ] Bullet groups expose evidence, decisions, timing, or action; otherwise the slide uses a stronger visual form.
- [ ] Section rhythm alternates heavy and simple slides.
- [ ] The deck feels like a professional risk briefing, not a course handout.
- [ ] Slides look premium, serious, technical, and conference-ready without decorative excess.
- [ ] No slide depends on template-like AI/security decoration instead of content structure.
- [ ] Public-download detail is visually subordinate to the spoken claim.
- [ ] Required disclaimer is placed immediately after the cover.

## 6. Dry-Run Checklist

Before run:

- [ ] Start timer.
- [ ] Record timestamps at slides 3, 6, 12, 17, 20, 23.
- [ ] Use the actual clicker path if available.
- [ ] Stand, do not rehearse seated.

During run:

- [ ] Do not restart after a mistake.
- [ ] Use reset sentences instead of apologizing.
- [ ] Mark where attention feels heavy.
- [ ] Mark every place where the speaker read instead of spoke.

After run:

- [ ] Record total time.
- [ ] Score all seven categories.
- [ ] Identify top three fixes only.
- [ ] Decide whether the next pass is structure, timing, delivery, or visual.

## 7. Live Rehearsal Checklist

- [ ] Run with projector or full-screen display.
- [ ] Use microphone if available.
- [ ] Stand at the expected stage distance from slides.
- [ ] Simulate first 30 seconds exactly.
- [ ] Simulate final 60 seconds exactly.
- [ ] Ask peer reviewer to write the talk arc from memory.
- [ ] Ask peer reviewer to name the strongest and weakest slide.
- [ ] Ask peer reviewer to score timing, delivery, and audience impact separately.
- [ ] Confirm no section depends on reading speaker notes verbatim.

## 10. Final Readiness Checklist

- [ ] Content finishes by `28:30`.
- [ ] Emergency `18:00-20:00` route is rehearsed once.
- [ ] Opening first sentence is memorized.
- [ ] Final line is memorized.
- [ ] Patch SLA explanation is under `1:35`.
- [ ] Slide 17 has silence after the patient-safety line.
- [ ] Slide 22 roadmap sounds executable, not aspirational.

## 11. Fix Order By Score Band

### If Score Is Below 70

Fix first:

1. Narrative spine: make model -> system -> evidence -> Patch SLA explicit.
2. Timing: cut detail until content finishes under `29:00`.
3. Content safety: remove unsupported claims and unsafe examples.
4. Slide role: delete or rewrite any slide whose purpose cannot be named.

Do not spend time on visual polish before the story and timing are stable.

### If Score Is 70-80

Fix first:

1. Strengthen transitions between sections.
2. Reduce dense slide reading.
3. Make product-scale examples sharper.
4. Preserve the close by cutting earlier detail.

Goal: move from understandable to conference-credible.

### If Score Is 80-90

Optimize:

1. Vocal variation and pause control.
2. Slide hierarchy and diagram clarity.
3. Memorability of core lines.
4. Peer-review evidence that audience remembers the product-scale map and Patch SLA.

Goal: move from strong to polished.

### What Distinguishes 90+ From Merely Good

- The opening creates trust within the first minute.
- The four-scale map becomes the audience's navigation tool.
- Technical sections feel useful instead of heavy.
- Patch SLA lands as the strongest practical insight.
- The close sounds inevitable and stops cleanly.
- Evaluators can cite evidence for high scores in all seven categories.
