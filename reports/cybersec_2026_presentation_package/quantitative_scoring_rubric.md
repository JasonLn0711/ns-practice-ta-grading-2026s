# CYBERSEC 2026 Quantitative Scoring Rubric

Purpose: convert the 100-point presentation rubric into an evidence-based evaluation system for design review, rehearsal review, peer review, and final readiness scoring.

This rubric must be used before slides are finalized, during rehearsal, and after each scored run. It is a design constraint, not a post-hoc checklist.

## 1. Scoring Philosophy

- Score observable performance and artifacts, not confidence alone.
- Every non-full category score must have a written reason.
- A high score requires evidence from slides, spoken delivery, timing, and audience effect.
- Do not award full credit for claims that are not visible in slide structure or audible in the rehearsal.
- Penalize repeated small defects if they create a pattern that affects audience cognition or speaker credibility.
- Protect the conference context: professional seriousness, technical substance, time control, and practical usefulness.
- Treat non-actionable advice, classroom-style delivery, unjustified slide recommendations, and template-like slide structure as scoreable defects, not taste preferences.

| Category | Points |
| --- | ---: |
| Structure and Narrative Design | 20 |

## 3. Detailed Category Rubrics

### 3.1 Structure and Narrative Design - 20 Points

| Subcriterion | Points | Full-Credit Standard |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Opening hook effectiveness | 4 | First minute establishes topic, stakes, and speaker credibility without wasting time |
| Logical progression | 5 | The arc clearly moves from model -> system -> evidence -> testing -> Patch SLA -> first 90 days |
| Transition clarity | 4 | Each section transition explains why the next idea is necessary |
| One-slide-one-message discipline | 4 | Every slide has one controlling claim and no filler role |
| Conclusion strength | 3 | Close restates the thesis, gives next action, and ends cleanly |

Scoring levels:

| Level | Score Range | Observable Performance |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Excellent | 18-20 | Audience can retell the talk arc and final takeaway without prompting |
| Strong | 15-17 | Clear structure with minor transition or closing weakness |
| Acceptable | 12-14 | Understandable sequence, but several sections feel loosely connected |
| Weak | 7-11 | Talk feels like a list of topics; thesis appears only intermittently |
| Poor | 0-6 | No coherent arc; audience cannot explain why sections appear in this order |

### 3.2 Content Depth and Value - 20 Points

| Subcriterion | Points | Full-Credit Standard |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Technical substance | 5 | Correctly explains AI SaMD risk, architecture scope, testing, SBOM, vulnerability workflow, and Patch SLA |
| Relevance to audience | 4 | Executives, engineers, PMs, regulatory teams, and clinical stakeholders each hear a clear reason to care |
| Insight density | 4 | Provides practical distinctions, not only definitions; examples include model vs system, white-box vs black-box, finding vs decision |
| Evidence/examples | 4 | Uses credible public/regulatory anchors and safe product examples without private details |
| Audience appropriateness | 3 | Technical depth is serious but accessible to mixed conference audience |

Scoring levels:

| Level | Score Range | Observable Performance |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Excellent | 18-20 | Technical listeners hear substance and non-specialists still understand the operating model |
| Strong | 15-17 | Mostly substantive, with one area needing sharper evidence or example |
| Acceptable | 12-14 | Useful but uneven; some sections are descriptive instead of insight-driven |
| Weak | 7-11 | Relies on framework names, risk slogans, or surface-level definitions |
| Poor | 0-6 | Inaccurate, unsupported, unsafe, or not conference-ready |

### 3.3 Stage Rhythm and Time Control - 15 Points

| Subcriterion | Points | Full-Credit Standard |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Pacing variation | 3 | Speaker varies speed between thesis, technical detail, examples, and close |
| Segment timing discipline | 4 | Normal run finishes content by `28:30` and protects `1:30` buffer |
| Transition fluidity | 3 | Movement between sections is smooth and does not require apology or restart |
| Cognitive load balancing | 3 | Heavy regulatory/technical sections are followed by simplification beats |
| Recovery beats | 2 | Speaker uses reset sentences and pauses when attention may drop |

Scoring levels:

| Level | Score Range | Observable Performance |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Excellent | 14-15 | Finishes on time with natural variation and no rushed ending |
| Strong | 12-13 | Minor timing drift but closing remains intact |
| Acceptable | 9-11 | Understandable pacing, but several sections drag or rush |
| Weak | 5-8 | Overtime risk, monotone pacing, or obvious slide reading |
| Poor | 0-4 | Timing failure damages core message or forces the close to be skipped |

### 3.4 Delivery Quality (Language and Voice Expression) - 15 Points

| Subcriterion | Points | Full-Credit Standard |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Clarity | 3 | Key concepts are explained in concise, understandable language |
| Wording precision | 3 | Uses technical terms correctly and avoids inflated claims |
| Vocal variation | 3 | Voice changes for contrast, emphasis, technical explanation, and close |
| Confidence | 3 | Speaker sounds controlled, not defensive or rushed |
| Verbal efficiency | 3 | No habitual filler; dense slides are not read line by line |

Scoring levels:

| Level | Score Range | Observable Performance |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Excellent | 14-15 | Speech sounds polished, precise, and natural under conference pressure |
| Strong | 12-13 | Clear and credible with minor filler or uneven phrasing |
| Acceptable | 9-11 | Understandable but sometimes wordy, flat, or translation-heavy |
| Weak | 5-8 | Frequent filler, apologetic phrasing, or unclear technical explanation |
| Poor | 0-4 | Delivery prevents comprehension or undermines credibility |

### 3.5 Visual Design of Slides - 10 Points

| Subcriterion | Points | Full-Credit Standard |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Clarity | 2 | Main point is visible within three seconds |
| Consistency | 2 | Typography, hierarchy, color, and diagram style feel coherent |
| Supportiveness to meaning | 2 | Visuals help audience understand architecture, process, contrast, or timing |
| Premium appearance | 2 | Slides look professional and restrained, not classroom-like or over-decorated |
| Non-template layout choices | 2 | Layouts reflect the content's cognitive job rather than repeating one pattern |

Scoring levels:

| Level | Score Range | Observable Performance |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Excellent | 9-10 | Slides feel conference-ready and make complex ideas easier |
| Strong | 8 | Mostly clear with a few dense or visually plain slides |
| Acceptable | 6-7 | Functional but uneven; several slides need hierarchy improvement |
| Weak | 3-5 | Text-heavy, cluttered, or inconsistent enough to affect attention |
| Poor | 0-2 | Slides actively obstruct comprehension or credibility |

### 3.6 Stage Presence - 10 Points

| Subcriterion | Points | Full-Credit Standard |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Eye contact | 2 | Speaker addresses audience, not the slide deck |
| Posture | 2 | Stable, open, controlled posture throughout |
| Gesture control | 2 | Gestures clarify architecture/process and do not distract |
| Composure | 2 | Handles timing, slide changes, and small mistakes calmly |
| Authority | 2 | Presence signals expertise without arrogance |

Scoring levels:

| Level | Score Range | Observable Performance |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Excellent | 9-10 | Speaker appears fully prepared and credible on a professional stage |
| Strong | 8 | Good control with minor stage or gesture issues |
| Acceptable | 6-7 | Generally steady but occasionally slide-bound or small in energy |
| Weak | 3-5 | Frequent fidgeting, weak eye contact, or uncertain posture |
| Poor | 0-2 | Stage behavior substantially reduces trust |

### 3.7 Audience Impact - 10 Points

| Subcriterion | Points | Full-Credit Standard |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Memorability | 2 | Audience remembers at least two core lines or mental models |
| Relevance felt by audience | 2 | Audience can connect the talk to their role or organization |
| Engagement maintenance | 2 | Attention is sustained through heavy sections |
| Practical usefulness | 2 | Audience leaves with a clear first step |
| Final impression | 2 | Closing feels decisive and worth remembering |

Scoring levels:

| Level | Score Range | Observable Performance |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Excellent | 9-10 | Audience leaves with a memorable model, practical action, and trust in the speaker |
| Strong | 8 | Strong practical value with minor memorability or close weakness |
| Acceptable | 6-7 | Useful but not especially distinctive |
| Weak | 3-5 | Interesting moments exist but no clear next action or memory |
| Poor | 0-2 | Audience cannot say what changed after the talk |

## 4. Evidence-Based Scoring Instructions

Evaluators should record evidence from four sources:

1. Slide artifact: visible title, layout, diagram, density, section logic.
2. Spoken performance: phrasing, transitions, technical explanation, vocal control.
3. Timing record: actual timestamps at slides 3, 6, 12, 17, 20, 23.
4. Audience simulation or peer feedback: what was remembered, where attention dropped, what felt useful.

Rules:

- Do not give full Structure and Narrative Design if the evaluator cannot state the story arc in one sentence.
- Do not give full Content Depth and Value if FDA/TFDA/NIST are mentioned without operational translation.
- Do not give full Stage Rhythm and Time Control if the close is rushed or the talk exceeds `30:00`.
- Do not give full Delivery Quality if the speaker reads dense bullets instead of speaking the controlling message.
- Do not give full Visual Design if any major slide requires the audience to read paragraphs during speech.
- Do not give full Stage Presence if the speaker repeatedly turns away for key lines.
- Do not give full Audience Impact if the evaluator cannot name the product-scale map, Patch SLA, or `30/60/90` plan afterward.

## 5. Severity-Based Deduction Model

| Severity | Typical Deduction | Definition | Examples |
| --- | ---: | --- | --- |
| Minor | `0.5-1` | Local defect; does not change the audience's understanding | One awkward transition, one dense slide label, one filler phrase pattern |
| Moderate | `1-3` | Repeated or section-level defect; affects comprehension or rhythm | Regulatory section feels long; technical term is unclear; close lacks pause |
| Major | `3-6` | Damages a core scoring category | Product-scale map is unclear; Patch SLA is rushed; visuals are frequently overloaded |
| Critical | `6+` | Breaks conference credibility or safety | Unsafe private example, materially incorrect regulatory claim, major overtime |

Deduction discipline:

- Deduct once for a root cause, then note cross-category effects.
- Do not double-penalize a single dense slide unless it affects both visual comprehension and spoken rhythm.
- A critical issue should be fixed before optimizing any 90+ polish item.
- Record the evidence line, slide number, or timestamp for every major or critical deduction.

### Quality-Contract Deduction Triggers

Use these triggers when the talk violates the conference quality contract.

| Trigger | Primary Category | Severity Range | Scoring Rule |
| --- | --- | ---: | --- |
| Non-actionable recommendation in review package | Structure and Narrative Design | Minor to moderate | Deduct if the recommendation cannot be converted into a slide, timing, delivery, or scoring action |
| Classroom-style speech framing | Delivery Quality (Language and Voice Expression) | Moderate to major | Deduct when the speaker sounds like presenting a report rather than briefing a professional audience |
| Bullet accumulation without evidence or decision value | Visual Design of Slides | Moderate to major | Deduct when bullets do not expose evidence, action, sequence, or tradeoff |
| Slide recommendation lacks justification | Structure and Narrative Design | Moderate | Deduct when a slide objective, audience understanding, visual form, or rubric support is missing |
| Major suggestion lacks rubric tie | Relevant affected category | Moderate to major | Deduct from the category the suggestion claims to improve; if none is clear, deduct from Structure |
| Template-like AI/security visual pattern | Visual Design of Slides | Moderate to major | Deduct when visuals rely on decoration rather than architecture, evidence, process, or decision logic |
| Weak premium/technical signal | Content Depth and Value | Moderate | Deduct when the talk relies on slogans, hype, or superficial framework mentions |
| Pacing or memorability sacrificed for more content | Stage Rhythm and Time Control | Moderate to critical | Deduct when added detail causes rushed close, unclear section turns, or lost Patch SLA memory |

## 8. Score Interpretation

| Total Score | Meaning | Required Response |
| ---: | --- | --- |
| `90-100` | Conference-ready, high credibility | Optimize polish, silence, and final memory |
| `80-89` | Strong but not fully premium | Fix rhythm, slide density, and closing authority |
| `70-79` | Passable but risky for CYBERSEC | Repair structure, timing, and practical value before visual polish |
| `<70` | Not ready | Redesign narrative spine and cut content before rehearsing again |
