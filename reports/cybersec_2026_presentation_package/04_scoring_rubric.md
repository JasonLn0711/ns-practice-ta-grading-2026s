# 04 Scoring Rubric

Purpose: provide a strict, auditable `100`-point evaluation framework for the CYBERSEC 2026 talk. Use this after every full rehearsal and after any major deck revision.

## 1. Score Allocation

| Category | Points |
| --- | ---: |
| Structure and Narrative Design | 20 |
| Content Depth and Value | 20 |
| Stage Rhythm and Time Control | 15 |
| Delivery Quality | 15 |
| Visual Design | 10 |
| Stage Presence | 10 |
| Audience Impact | 10 |
| Total | 100 |

Scoring rule: award points only for observed performance evidence. Do not score intention, preparation effort, or file completeness unless it is visible in the delivered talk.

## 2. Category Subcriteria

### 2.1 Structure And Narrative Design - 20

| Subcriterion | Points | Excellent Evidence | Deduct When |
| --- | ---: | --- | --- |
| Opening hook effectiveness | 4 | Slide 3 reframes the whole talk by `2:20`; audience understands `not model, trust system`. | Opening begins with biography, agenda, or slow setup. |
| Logical progression | 5 | Arc is clear: product scale -> evidence -> governance -> testing -> Patch SLA -> implementation. | Sections feel interchangeable or framework-heavy. |
| Transition clarity | 4 | Each transition names why the next section exists. | Speaker says `next` without explaining the logic. |
| One-slide-one-message discipline | 4 | Each slide has one claim and one visual object. | Slides contain several competing points. |
| Conclusion strength | 3 | Close reinforces trust-before-audit and stops cleanly. | Ending introduces new content or administrative clutter. |

### 2.2 Content Depth And Value - 20

| Subcriterion | Points | Excellent Evidence | Deduct When |
| --- | ---: | --- | --- |
| Technical substance | 5 | Product scale, AI stack, testing roles, Patch SLA, and evidence artifacts are specific. | Talk remains at slogan level. |
| Regulatory translation | 4 | FDA 524B and FDA 2025 are translated into monitor, patch, disclose, SBOM, and traceability. | Regulations are only named. |
| Relevance to target audience | 4 | Medical-device, security, regulatory, and management listeners each get a decision model. | Talk fits only one narrow audience segment. |
| Evidence/examples | 4 | Examples are public-safe and tied to artifacts, controls, findings, or decisions. | Examples are vague, confidential, or fear-driven. |
| Audience appropriateness | 3 | Technical terms are clear without oversimplifying. | Talk becomes either too shallow or too specialized. |

### 2.3 Stage Rhythm And Time Control - 15

| Subcriterion | Points | Excellent Evidence | Deduct When |
| --- | ---: | --- | --- |
| Pacing variation | 3 | Slow memory slides, efficient technical comparisons, strong close. | Same pace for all slides. |
| Segment timing discipline | 4 | Slide 7 ends near `14:00`; slide 14 begins by `27:40`; talk ends by `28:30`. | Regulatory middle expands and close gets rushed. |
| Transition fluidity | 3 | Speaker moves without dead air or mechanical outline language. | Transitions feel like file navigation. |
| Cognitive load balancing | 3 | Heavy slides 6-7 are followed by concrete product-scale slides. | Dense explanation stacks without recovery. |
| Recovery beats | 2 | Slide 10 and slide 14 use silence effectively. | Speaker fills every pause. |

### 2.4 Delivery Quality - 15

| Subcriterion | Points | Excellent Evidence | Deduct When |
| --- | ---: | --- | --- |
| Clarity | 3 | Sentences are short and meaning is easy to follow. | Speaker reads or tangles clauses. |
| Wording precision | 3 | Uses `evidence`, `finding`, `decision`, `retest`, `Patch SLA` consistently. | Terms shift or remain undefined. |
| Vocal variation | 3 | Strong lines have tonal contrast and silence. | Voice stays flat. |
| Confidence | 3 | Speaker sounds prepared and does not apologize for complexity. | Hedging weakens authority. |
| Verbal efficiency | 3 | No filler detours; cut markers are used under time pressure. | Speaker adds examples after slide 10. |

### 2.5 Visual Design - 10

| Subcriterion | Points | Excellent Evidence | Deduct When |
| --- | ---: | --- | --- |
| Clarity | 2 | Headline meaning visible within three seconds. | Audience must read paragraphs. |
| Consistency | 2 | Diagram grammar is consistent across scale and evidence slides. | Layout changes randomly. |
| Supportiveness to meaning | 2 | Visuals carry logic: ladder, chain, stack, comparison, flow, roadmap. | Visuals decorate without explaining. |
| Premium appearance | 2 | Restrained, high-contrast, technical, conference-ready. | Looks like a template dump or marketing flyer. |
| Non-template layout choices | 2 | Memory slides and process slides have distinct composition. | All slides look like bullet sheets. |

### 2.6 Stage Presence - 10

| Subcriterion | Points | Excellent Evidence | Deduct When |
| --- | ---: | --- | --- |
| Eye contact | 2 | Speaker looks at audience on strong lines. | Speaker reads from slides. |
| Posture | 2 | Stable stance, controlled movement. | Wandering or turning away during key claims. |
| Gesture control | 2 | Gestures explain scale, chain, and flow. | Gestures are restless or unrelated. |
| Composure | 2 | Speaker handles time pressure with planned cuts. | Rushing or visible panic. |
| Authority | 2 | Tone is calm, precise, and credible. | Performance feels under-rehearsed. |

### 2.7 Audience Impact - 10

| Subcriterion | Points | Excellent Evidence | Deduct When |
| --- | ---: | --- | --- |
| Memorability | 2 | Audience can recall at least three anchors after the talk. | No durable phrase or model remains. |
| Relevance felt by audience | 2 | Audience sees how the talk applies to their product or organization. | Talk feels abstract. |
| Engagement maintenance | 2 | Attention recovers after dense sections. | Attention drops after regulatory block. |
| Practical usefulness | 2 | `30 / 60 / 90` and Patch SLA are executable. | Ending lacks next action. |
| Final impression | 2 | Close is clean and serious. | Ending is rushed or diluted. |

## 3. Scoring Bands

| Band | Score | Meaning | Required Fix |
| --- | ---: | --- | --- |
| Excellent | `90-100` | Conference-ready; strong narrative, technical credibility, timing, and memorability. | Polish delivery and slide-level clarity only. |
| Strong | `80-89` | Solid professional talk with a few visible weaknesses. | Fix the lowest category before adding content. |
| Acceptable | `70-79` | Understandable but not yet high-impact. | Repair structure, timing, and slide density first. |
| Weak | `60-69` | Content exists but delivery or structure is not conference-ready. | Simplify deck and rebuild narrative path. |
| Poor | `<60` | Not ready for a professional audience. | Rebuild from thesis, map, evidence chain, Patch SLA. |

## 4. Evidence-Based Scoring Rules

1. If the evaluator cannot state the talk arc in one sentence, Structure and Narrative Design cannot exceed `14/20`.
2. If FDA/TFDA/NIST are named without operational translation, Content Depth and Value cannot exceed `13/20`.
3. If slide 14 starts after `28:00`, Stage Rhythm and Time Control cannot exceed `10/15`.
4. If the speaker reads slide text for more than one major slide, Delivery Quality cannot exceed `10/15`.
5. If any major slide requires paragraph reading, Visual Design cannot exceed `7/10`.
6. If the speaker turns away during `Cyber Safety is Patient Safety` or the Patch SLA line, Stage Presence cannot exceed `7/10`.
7. If the audience cannot recall product scale, Patch SLA, or `30 / 60 / 90`, Audience Impact cannot exceed `6/10`.

## 5. Severity-Based Deduction Model

| Severity | Deduction | Use When |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Minor | `0.25-0.5` | Small wording, timing, or visual issue that does not affect comprehension. |
| Moderate | `0.75-1.5` | Noticeable issue that weakens one slide, transition, or delivery moment. |
| Major | `2-4` | Issue damages a whole section or scoring category. |
| Critical | `5+` | Issue breaks the talk promise, violates safety boundary, or causes serious time failure. |

Safety boundary rule: any actual private hospital/client detail, raw student record, proprietary code, exploit recipe, or patent-sensitive implementation mechanic shown in the public deck is at least a major deduction and may be critical depending on exposure.
